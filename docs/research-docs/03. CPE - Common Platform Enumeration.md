CPE, or **Common Platform Enumeration**, is the industry-standard way to identify hardware, software, and operating systems in a machine-readable format. Think of it as a **"barcode for software products"** that security scanners and vulnerability databases use to automatically know exactly what a "vulnerable product" is.

### 1. What does a CPE string look like?

The most common version is **CPE 2.3**. It is a formatted string separated by colons that identifies the specific product being discussed.

**Example:**
`cpe:2.3:a:redhat:enterprise_linux:9.0:*:*:*:*:*:*:*`

Breaking down those parts:

* `cpe:2.3`: The CPE version.
* `a`: **Part** (Type of product: `a`=Application, `o`=Operating System, `h`=Hardware).
* `redhat`: **Vendor** (The organization that made it).
* `enterprise_linux`: **Product** (The name).
* `9.0`: **Version** (The specific release).
* `*` (Wildcards): These represent fields not being specified (like "update," "edition," "language," etc.).

### 2. Why is this used in CVE reports?

If a CVE record simply said "This bug affects Red Hat Linux," it would be ambiguous. Does it affect RHEL 7? RHEL 8? RHEL 9?
By using a **CPE**, the CVE record explicitly tells a machine: "This bug affects the product identified as `cpe:2.3:o:redhat:enterprise_linux:9.0`."

When your customers run a security scanner (like *Trivy* or *Grype*), the scanner looks at their own inventory, generates a list of CPEs for their installed software, and compares them against the CPEs listed in the CVE record. If they match, the scanner flags the vulnerability.

### 3. How to use this for your B2B software

Since you are building enterprise software, you have two roles regarding CPEs:

#### A. When documenting your own software (The "Affected" list)

In your internal security records or the VEX files you provide to customers, you should define your product using a CPE-like structure.

* **Best Practice:** Many modern companies are moving toward **PURL (Package URL)** because it is often easier for developers to manage than CPEs. However, **CPEs are still the mandatory language for NVD/CVE compliance**.
* **Consistency is Key:** Pick a naming convention for your vendor and product name and stick to it. If you call yourself `acme_corp:secure_data_vault` in one report, don't call it `acme:vault` in the next.

#### B. The "CPE Dictionary"

You can search the [official NIST CPE Dictionary](https://nvd.nist.gov/products/cpe) to see how other companies (like Red Hat or Microsoft) register their products. You might eventually want to register your own products there so that when you publish a CVE, your customers' scanners recognize your product name automatically.

### Summary Checklist for your team:

1. **Standardization:** Ensure your CI/CD pipeline knows how to map your internal product version to a CPE-2.3 string.
2. **Granularity:** Be as specific as possible. If a bug only affects the "Enterprise Edition" of your software, your CPE string should reflect that using the `sw_edition` or `other` fields in the CPE format.
3. **VEX Mapping:** Your VEX report will use these same CPE strings to tell your customers, "This product (identified by this CPE) is `known_not_affected` by this CVE."

**Pro-Tip:** If your software has a lot of components (like different microservices), don't just assign a CPE to the "whole product." Assign CPEs to the **individual modules or binaries**. This allows you to issue much more precise VEX reports, which your customers will love because it prevents them from having to patch their entire system when only one small, non-critical service was affected.
