The CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures) program is a collaborative effort involving several layers of responsibility. No single entity "owns" every vulnerability; instead, a federation of trusted organizations works together.

### 1. The Manager: The MITRE Corporation

**MITRE** is the primary organization that manages the infrastructure and operations of the CVE Program. They are a private, non-profit organization funded by the U.S. government (specifically CISA).
- **Role:** They maintain the "CVE List," provide the technological infrastructure for recording and publishing vulnerabilities, and act as the final authority for the program’s rules.

### 2. The Assigners: CVE Numbering Authorities (CNAs)

The bulk of the "day-to-day" work of identifying and cataloging is handled by **CNAs**. These are over 100 authorized organizations worldwide that have the authority to assign CVE IDs to vulnerabilities within their specific "scope."

- **Who are they?**
    - **Software Vendors:** (e.g., Red Hat, Microsoft, Google, Apple) assign IDs for their own products.
    - **Security Research Firms:** (e.g., Rapid7, Cisco, Trend Micro) assign IDs they discover in various systems.
    - **Open Source Projects:** (e.g., Linux Foundation, Apache) assign IDs for their respective codebases.
    - **Bug Bounty Providers:** (e.g., HackerOne) facilitate the assignment for vulnerabilities reported through their platforms.

- **Why decentralize?** By allowing vendors and researchers to assign their own IDs, the process happens much faster and stays closer to the initial disclosure.
    

### 3. The "Enrichers": NIST and the National Vulnerability Database (NVD)

While MITRE/CNAs handle the **identification** and **naming** (the cataloging), **NIST** (National Institute of Standards and Technology) handles much of the **enrichment** via the **NVD**.
- **Role:** Once a CVE is published, the NVD team analyzes it to provide additional technical details, such as the **CVSS score** (the severity rating) and the **CWE** (Common Weakness Enumeration, which describes the _type_ of bug, like "Buffer Overflow").

### Summary of the Workflow

1. **Discovery:** A researcher (or internal team) finds a bug.
2. **Assignment:** They contact the relevant **CNA** (or MITRE if no CNA exists for that software) to reserve a **CVE ID**.
3. **Definition:** The CNA writes the description and provides the references, effectively "cataloging" it.
4. **Publication:** The entry is published to the central **CVE List** (cve.org) managed by MITRE.
5. **Enrichment:** **NIST/NVD** picks up the entry, analyzes it, and adds the "risk score" (CVSS) and other technical metadata.

**Why this matters for your software team:**

As your team builds its own B2B commercial software, you may eventually want to **become a CNA**. This allows you to manage the security disclosure process for your own product directly, rather than waiting for an external entity to assign an ID to a vulnerability found in your software. It is a sign of high security maturity that enterprise customers often look for during the procurement process.
