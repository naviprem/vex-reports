
When a vulnerability is found in an **upstream dependency** (a third-party library or package not owned by the vendor), Red Hat’s responsibility—and your responsibility as a B2B software provider—shifts from "original researcher" to **"integrator and validator."**

Even if Red Hat didn't write the code for the vulnerable library, they are responsible for their "integrated distribution" of that software. Here is how they handle it and how you should think about your own responsibility.

### 1. The Vendor’s Responsibility

If Red Hat ships a product (like RHEL or OpenShift) that includes a vulnerable upstream dependency (e.g., a vulnerable version of `OpenSSL` or `glibc`), they are **still responsible** for the security of that product.

Their VEX responsibility is to tell their customers:

* **The "Integrated Reality":** "We know this CVE exists in the upstream package. We have analyzed our specific build, and it [does/does not] affect our product."
* **The Remediation Path:** "If we are affected, here is when we will ship a patched version of our package."

### 2. How the VEX Workflow changes for Dependencies

When the vulnerability is in a dependency, the vendor essentially performs a **"Security Impact Assessment"** on their own build:

* **If the vulnerability is not exploitable in their build:** They publish a VEX report with status `known_not_affected` and a justification (e.g., `vulnerable_code_not_present` or `not_in_execute_path`). This is crucial because it keeps customers from panicking over every single CVE found in a massive open-source library.
* **If they are vulnerable:** They acknowledge the status as `known_affected` and eventually `fixed` once they patch their own downstream version of that package.

### 3. Your Responsibility as a B2B Enterprise Software Provider

As you build your own software, your customers will treat you exactly as they treat Red Hat. If a CVE is found in a core library you use (like a logging framework or a database driver), **your customers will expect a statement from you.**

#### Your "Responsibility Roadmap":

1. **Monitor Upstream:** Use tools like *Dependency-Track* or *Snyk* to track vulnerabilities in your third-party dependencies automatically.
2. **Triage/Analyze:** When an upstream CVE is announced, have your security team (or lead architect) quickly determine: *Is our code actually calling the vulnerable function?*
3. **Publish VEX:**
* If you are **not affected**, publish a VEX file as soon as possible. This is your "get out of jail free" card; it tells your customers they don't need to waste time updating their security teams on a non-issue.
* If you **are affected**, tell your customers: "We are `under_investigation`," and then provide a timeline for your own patch.



### Why this creates "Trust" in B2B

The real value of VEX is in **curation**.

If you are a B2B vendor, your customers are likely being bombarded with hundreds of "High" and "Critical" vulnerability alerts from their automated scanners. If you provide them with a machine-readable VEX file that says, "We have verified that *this* specific CVE does not affect our software because of *this* technical reason," you are effectively becoming a **trusted filter** for their security operations.

**Summary of Responsibility:**

* **Upstream Maintainers:** Responsible for fixing the *raw bug*.
* **You (the Vendor):** Responsible for determining the *impact on your customers* and communicating that through a signed, machine-readable VEX report.

If you don't publish these reports, your customers are forced to perform their own (often inaccurate) security assessments of your product, which leads to procurement friction and "false-positive" security alerts that can delay your sales or renewals.
