The short answer is **you must do both.**

If you only scan at build time, you will have a "blind spot" for vulnerabilities that are disclosed *after* you have shipped a version of your software. In B2B enterprise software, your customers are running your code 24/7/365, regardless of when you last pushed an update.

Here is how to structure your pipeline to cover both phases:

### 1. The Build-Time Pipeline (The "Shift-Left" Gate)

This ensures you don't release "known broken" software.

* **When:** Triggers on every Pull Request (PR) and Merge Request.
* **Goal:** Quality Control and Developer Awareness.
* **Action:** If a PR introduces a new dependency with a "High" or "Critical" CVE, the build **fails** (or warns the developer) unless there is a pre-approved exception.
* **VEX Output:** This is where you create the "initial" VEX record for that release.

### 2. The Continuous Monitoring Pipeline (The "Scan-Anytime" Loop)

This is the "always-on" security monitor for your production software.

* **When:** Runs on a daily schedule (e.g., at 03:00 AM) against all currently supported/deployed versions.
* **Goal:** Responding to "Zero-Day" or newly disclosed vulnerabilities.
* **The Workflow:**
* **Continuous SBOM Ingestion:** You should maintain a database of your deployed software's SBOMs.
* **Periodic Re-Correlation:** Every day, your system fetches the latest CVE feeds from sources like the NVD or OSV and matches them against the SBOMs of your *already-deployed* products.
* **Continuous Reachability:** If a new high-severity CVE is reported for a library you use, the system re-runs the reachability analysis against the archived source code/binaries of that specific version.


* **VEX Output:** If the daily scan finds a new CVE, and your reachability engine confirms it is `not_affected`, your system should **automatically update and re-publish your VEX report.**

---

### Why the "Everyday" Scan is Mandatory for B2B

1. **The "Lurking" Vulnerability:** You might have shipped a product six months ago that was perfectly secure. Today, a new vulnerability is discovered in an old library that you included. Your customers will receive alerts from their own scanners and immediately look to *you* for a VEX statement. If you haven't scanned daily, you will be caught flat-footed.
2. **Maintaining Trust:** Proactive communication is better than reactive communication. If your customer sees a critical alert and you have *already* updated your VEX report on your portal to say "Known Not Affected - Not Reachable" before they even call your support team, you cement your reputation as a high-maturity security partner.
3. **Customer Compliance:** Your customers' compliance scanners (like *Dependency-Track*) will query your security portal every day. If your portal is stale, their scanners will keep flagging your product as "vulnerable," leading to unnecessary friction in your support and sales relationships.

### Implementation Strategy for the Lead Architect

* **Decouple SBOM from Build:** Don't just generate an SBOM during the build and store it as a build artifact. **Push that SBOM to a central database** (like Dependency-Track or a custom database) as part of your release process.
* **Event-Driven Trigger:** If a major new CVE is released (e.g., another *Log4j*), your monitoring pipeline should be able to trigger an "On-Demand Scan" across your entire portfolio of deployed products within minutes.
* **The "Security Dashboard":** As the architect, you should have a single pane of glass that shows:
* Which products are currently deployed.
* Which CVEs are "open" vs "mitigated" (via VEX).
* The last time each product was scanned.



**My recommendation:** Start by automating the build-time checks for developers, but dedicate your next project phase to the **Continuous Monitoring Pipeline**. In the B2B space, the latter is what prevents "fire drills" when a major vulnerability hits the news.
